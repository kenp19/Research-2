Technology Milestones

Key milestones in the development of computers and logical devices

Phone Background




Mobile telephone has a long history that started off with experiments of communications from and to moving vehicle rather than handheld devices.
The first successful mobile phone service was offered to first class passengers on the Deutsche Reichsbahn. Dr Martin Cooper
general manager at Motorola communications system division made the first public mobile phone call on a device that weighed 1.1Kg in 1973.
The world’s first SMS message was sent in the UK by Neil Papworth, a developer for a telecom contractor.
The text message read “Merry Christmas” and was sent to Richard Jarvis, a director at Vodafone.
The first downloadable content sold to mobile phones was a ringtone, by Finland’s Radiolinja.
Emojis were introduced by Shigetaka Kurita in Japan. Around this time, because of the shots fired in a supermarket price war,
you could pick up a mobile phone for just under £40. The first blackberry phone was unveiled in 1999.
It was known for its easy email service. This led to 83% of users reading and responding to work emails all the time.



Macintosh


The Macintosh, or Mac, is a series of several lines of personal computers, manufactured by Apple.
The first Macintosh was introduced January 24, 1984 by Steve Jobs.
The Macintosh project started in the late 1970s with Jef Raskin, an Apple employee.
He originally wanted to name the computer after his favorite type of apple, the McIntosh, but it had to be changed regarding to legal reasons.
The first Macintosh board had 64 kilobytes. It was designed by Burrell Smith.
Apple spent $2.5 million to buy all 39 of the advertising pages. The limitations of the first Mac soon became clear.
It had very little memory, even compared with other personal computers in 1984.
Apple increased the Mac’s memory in October 1985, but it was inconvenient and difficult to expand.
Apple then released the Macintosh Plus on January 1986 for $2600. The Macintosh was targeted for two primary markets: knowledge-workers and students.



Windows


The original Windows 1 was released in November 1985 and it was Microsoft’s first attempt at a graphical user interface in 16-bit.
To help users become familiar with this odd input system, Microsoft included a game, Reversi,
which relied on mouse control, not the keyboard.
This was aimed to get people used to moving the mouse around and clicking onscreen elements.
Microsoft’s Windows 2 replaced Windows 1 in December 1987, two years after its release.
Microsoft Word and Excel made their first appearances running on Windows 2.
Windows 3 was the first version to see more widespread success. It was considered a challenger to Apple’s Macintosh.
Windows 3 introduced the ability to run MS-DOS programs in windows. This brought multitasking to legacy programs.
Windows 95 arrived in August 1955 and it brought the first ever Start button and Start menu.
Windows 10 – despite being the ninth version of Windows – is designed to unify all Windows platforms across multiple devices.



Television


Electronic television was first successfully introduced in San Francisco in September 1927.
The system was designed by Philo Taylor Farnsworth. The company that dominated the radio business in the United States,
invested $50 million in the development of electronic television.
Early television was primitive. All the action at that first televised baseball game had
to be captured by one single camera. Television’s progress was further slowed by a struggle over wavelength allocations
with the new FM radio. In 1953-1955, television programming began to take some steps away from radio formats.
In the 1980s, home videocassette recorders became widely available. The viewers gained ability to record and replay programs.
The number of cable networks grew throughout the 1980s and then exploded in the 1990s.
Although the population of the US was continued to grow, the Nielon Media Research Company estimated
that fewer people watched the highly publicized final episode of Seinfield.



Cellphones


Cell phones were originally created so that people could talk while they drove. These phones were initially
called “car phones”. The world’s first cell phone was launched in 1983. This was the Motorola DynaTAC 800x.
This was priced at around $4,000. The talk time before dying lasted for about 30 minutes.
The Motorola was the size of a foot long sub from Subway. Although the phone size was large,
it was still considered to be the most portable telephone ever made. A human could call someone without the constraints of wires or portable phone holders.
The original Mobile Telephone Service was soon supplanted by Improved Mobile Telephone Service. In an effort to compete with AT&T,
independent American telephone companies introduced the Radio Common Carrier. This system used frequencies near
the IMTS frequencies. Europe, like America, developed its own series of mobile radio networks.
In 1973, 10 years before a cell was released, the first cell phone was made by Martin Cooper.
